# Alesvia — Partner Deck Visual Spec

This document defines how the partner decks should work visually.

It covers:

- slide hierarchy
- graphic structure
- text density
- layout rules
- how to visually differentiate the general deck from the institutional and funder deck

The design goal is not “startup deck.” It is editorial, composed, and institution-first.

---

## Global visual direction

### Overall feel

- serious
- modern
- editorial
- premium but not luxurious
- restrained rather than decorative

### Avoid

- startup pitch deck clichés
- excessive gradients
- overly dense data slides
- playful icon overload
- generic SaaS-style cards everywhere
- investor-deck hype language visuals

### Typography hierarchy

Use three levels consistently:

- **Level 1**: slide title
  Large serif or high-contrast editorial face
- **Level 2**: supporting statement or subtitle
  clean sans-serif, medium weight
- **Level 3**: bullets / proof lines / notes
  smaller sans-serif, light to regular weight

### Color hierarchy

- dark, stable base background
- warm accent for emphasis only
- light neutral text for primary reading
- muted text for secondary copy

### Grid rule

Use a simple 12-column or equivalent editorial grid.

- left side: thesis / title / positioning
- right side: proof / supporting structure / lists

Not every slide needs a split layout, but the deck should feel structurally intentional.

---

## Universal slide anatomy

Most slides should follow this hierarchy:

1. Eyebrow or slide label, optional
2. Primary headline
3. One supporting statement or framing paragraph
4. One main proof structure
5. Optional closing line

The proof structure can be:

- a short bullet cluster
- a 2-column comparison
- a 4-part model
- a restrained initiative list

## Deck A — General Partner Deck Visual Spec

### Slide 1 — Cover

Graphic hierarchy:

- top left or centered: `Alesvia`
- dominant headline: `Human autonomy is the baseline.`
- smaller supporting line below
- subtle atmospheric background only

Visual notes:

- keep the cover minimal
- no logos grid
- no busy graphic system
- one restrained visual motif is enough

### Slide 2 — Why Alesvia Exists

Graphic hierarchy:

- large headline on the left
- short framing paragraph underneath
- right side or lower section: 3 concise challenge bullets

Visual notes:

- use whitespace aggressively
- this slide should feel like a problem statement, not an explainer wall

### Slide 3 — The Core Thesis

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline dominant
- one bold central statement block
- supporting bullet list beneath or to the side

Visual notes:

- this is a thesis slide, so the central quote or principle should visually dominate
- use contrast and spacing rather than decoration

### Slide 4 — What We Build

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline at top
- 4-part grid beneath:
  - Research
  - Policy
  - Education
  - Implementation

Visual notes:

- each block should have equal weight
- do not over-style these as product cards
- they should feel like institutional functions

### Slide 5 — Initiative Ecosystem

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- short framing line
- initiative matrix or stacked list

Visual notes:

- use restrained grouping
- highlight 2-3 initiatives visually, keep the rest secondary
- avoid making this look like a feature catalog

### Slide 6 — Where We Fit

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline left
- partner categories right
- bottom or side strip: “translation gap” lines

Visual notes:

- this slide benefits from a 2-column structure
- categories should read fast

### Slide 7 — What We Are Looking For

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- short opening line
- collaboration priorities list
- closing line in muted emphasis

Visual notes:

- this slide should feel actionable
- keep the bullet list short and clean

### Slide 8 — Why Partner With Alesvia

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- 4-5 concise benefit statements

Visual notes:

- use one-line claims rather than long paragraphs
- this is a synthesis slide, so clarity matters more than novelty

### Slide 9 — Contact / Next Step

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- short invitation paragraph
- 3-4 concrete next-step bullets
- contact line at bottom

Visual notes:

- end with calm confidence
- do not use oversized CTA button graphics

## Deck B — Institutional & Funder Deck Visual Spec

This deck should feel slightly more severe, strategic, and structured than Deck A.

### Slide 1 — Cover

Graphic hierarchy:

- `Alesvia`
- dominant line: `Institutional infrastructure for human autonomy`
- smaller supporting line below

Visual notes:

- slightly more formal than the general deck cover
- less atmosphere, more authority

### Slide 2 — The Institutional Problem

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline at top
- short framing sentence
- 4 bullet problem stack beneath

Visual notes:

- this slide should feel systemic, not emotional
- use clear structure, not flourish

### Slide 3 — Why Autonomy Is the Right Frame

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- left: short explanation
- right: 4 proof bullets explaining why autonomy is the stronger standard

Visual notes:

- this slide benefits from a split layout
- make the frame feel decisive

### Slide 4 — The Gap in the Current Field

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- simple vertical list of field failures / fragmentation points
- one closing line at bottom

Visual notes:

- keep it crisp and diagnostic
- avoid sounding accusatory toward other actors

### Slide 5 — Alesvia’s Model

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- 4-part horizontal or 2x2 system model

Visual notes:

- this should be the cleanest systems slide in the deck
- use structural consistency to imply institutional maturity

### Slide 6 — Why This Matters for Funders

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- short leverage statement
- 4-point compounding-effects cluster
- optional bottom strip of priority domains

Visual notes:

- this slide should read like leverage, not philanthropy marketing

### Slide 7 — Why This Matters for Institutions

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- left: 1 short explanatory paragraph
- right: 5 practical ways Alesvia helps institutions

Visual notes:

- keep the right column practical and scannable

### Slide 8 — Initiative Platform

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- grouped initiative list by function or audience

Possible grouping:

- public behavior: Unplugged
- governance: Policy Lab, Watch
- literacy and education: Education, Proof
- implementation: Advisory, Compass
- professional practice: Mind

Visual notes:

- grouped layout is better here than a flat list
- this slide should show platform logic

### Slide 9 — What Partnership Can Look Like

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- collaboration modes list
- one short closing line

Visual notes:

- very concrete
- no vague “join us” tone

### Slide 10 — Why Now

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- 4 urgency points
- strong final line

Visual notes:

- visually this should feel like the highest-tension slide after the opening problem
- use spacing and emphasis, not alarmist graphics

### Slide 11 — Closing

Graphic hierarchy:

- dominant thesis line
- short 4-part supporting structure beneath
- one closing sentence

Visual notes:

- this should mirror slide 3 conceptually, but in operational language

### Slide 12 — Contact

Graphic hierarchy:

- headline
- 3-4 next-step bullets
- contact information anchored low on the slide

Visual notes:

- the final screen should feel executive and composed

## Content density rules

### Headline length

- preferred: 5 to 12 words
- maximum: around 16 words

### Supporting text

- one short paragraph or one short statement block
- avoid more than 45 to 60 words in one block

### Bullet count

- ideal: 3 to 5 bullets
- maximum: 6 on a slide

### Initiative lists

- if the list exceeds 6-8 visible items, use grouping or hierarchy

---

## Visual differentiation between the two decks

### General partner deck

- slightly warmer
- more relational
- more ecosystem-facing
- more emphasis on collaboration and fit

### Institutional and funder deck

- slightly stricter and more formal
- more systemic
- more leverage-oriented
- more emphasis on public-interest capacity and institutional timing

---

## Recommended build sequence

When converting docs into slides:

1. build Deck A cover, thesis, and closing first
2. build Deck B problem, model, and leverage slides second
3. keep one shared visual language across both decks
4. only after structure is stable, add any visual accents or atmospheric treatment
