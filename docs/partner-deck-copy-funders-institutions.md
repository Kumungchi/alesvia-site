# Alesvia — Institutional & Funder Deck Copy

This document turns the institutional and funder deck outline into ready-to-use presentation copy.

Audience:

- philanthropic funders
- public-interest funders
- institutional partners
- universities and research centers
- policy and governance actors
- strategic ecosystem allies

Tone:

- sharper than the general partner deck
- institution-first
- leverage-oriented
- calm, serious, and non-startup

---

## Slide 1 — Cover

### Title

Alesvia

### Subtitle

Institutional infrastructure for human autonomy

### Supporting line

Building public-interest capacity as AI becomes ordinary.

### Speaker note

Open with institutional seriousness, not inspiration. The point of this deck is that Alesvia is being built as a platform for durable public-interest capacity, not as a campaign, not as a product, and not as a temporary commentary project.

---

## Slide 2 — The Institutional Problem

### Title

AI adoption is accelerating faster than the infrastructure needed to govern it well.

### Body

Across sectors, institutions are being pushed to integrate AI before they have:

- literacy and training models
- usable governance frameworks
- practical implementation standards
- public-interest accountability mechanisms

The problem is not only technical capability. It is institutional lag under conditions of rapid adoption.

### Speaker note

This slide should make the problem legible at the system level. The key idea is that adoption is outpacing institutional preparedness.

---

## Slide 3 — Why Autonomy Is the Right Frame

### Title

Autonomy is the missing public-interest standard.

### Body

Generic “AI ethics” language is too broad to guide real decisions.

Autonomy is a stronger frame because it makes the problem concrete:

- how systems shape judgment
- how dependency becomes normalized
- how accountability shifts away from people
- how reflection and refusal become harder to sustain

Human autonomy is the baseline because it is the condition that allows institutions, citizens, and professionals to act meaningfully at all.

### Speaker note

This is the conceptual anchor of the deck. The goal is to show why Alesvia’s framing is more usable and more defensible than vague ethical language.

---

## Slide 4 — The Gap in the Current Field

### Title

Today’s field is fragmented.

### Body

- research often does not travel into practice
- policy often lacks implementation tools
- schools and institutions need operational guidance now
- investors and builders face weak public-interest standards
- civil society critique is often not matched by delivery capacity

### Closing line

Alesvia is built to close the gap between serious ideas and usable institutional action.

### Speaker note

Keep this slide disciplined. It should not sound like a complaint about other actors. It should sound like a structural diagnosis and a reason for Alesvia’s existence.

---

## Slide 5 — Alesvia’s Model

### Title

We convert ideas into standards, and standards into practice.

### Body

Alesvia works across four linked functions:

- **Research**
  Briefings, reports, and applied analysis on autonomy-related AI impacts.
- **Policy**
  Governance frameworks, implementation guidance, and institutional recommendations.
- **Education**
  Programs that strengthen literacy, judgment, and boundary-setting.
- **Implementation**
  Partner support for responsible deployment, evaluation, and ecosystem coordination.

### Speaker note

This is the operating model slide. It should make it clear that Alesvia is a platform, not a single intervention.

---

## Slide 6 — Why This Matters for Funders

### Title

This is leverage, not noise.

### Body

Support for Alesvia can compound across multiple domains at once:

- public research and narrative formation
- education and institutional literacy
- applied governance and implementation frameworks
- ecosystem-building in the Czech and European context

Rather than supporting one isolated project, a partner can help build a platform capable of shaping how multiple sectors respond to AI over time.

### Speaker note

This slide is for explaining multiplier effects. The message is that Alesvia is a capacity-building institution with cross-domain leverage.

---

## Slide 7 — Why This Matters for Institutions

### Title

Institutions need more than principles.

### Body

Alesvia helps partners:

- interpret emerging AI risks through the lens of autonomy
- translate ethics and policy into usable standards
- develop literacy and training capacity
- connect research with applied institutional needs
- create credible public-interest responses before the problem becomes harder to reverse

### Closing line

Our role is often translational: turning diffuse concerns into serious, adoptable structures.

### Speaker note

This slide should feel directly useful. It is where the audience should start imagining how Alesvia could fit into their own institutional reality.

---

## Slide 8 — Initiative Platform

### Title

One thesis, multiple practical fronts.

### Body

- **Unplugged**: healthy boundaries with conversational AI
- **Policy Lab**: legislative and institutional response
- **Education**: literacy and digital self-defense
- **Advisory**: ethical investment and deployment guidance
- **Mind**: mental health and AI practice
- **Proof**: evidence-based AI literacy
- **Compass**: ethical innovation guidance
- **Watch**: AI impact monitoring and public accountability

### Closing line

Together, these initiatives make Alesvia more than a think tank and more than a service provider. They make it a platform for sustained institutional action.

### Speaker note

As with the general deck, do not flatten every initiative into equal detail. Highlight only the ones that fit the room.
